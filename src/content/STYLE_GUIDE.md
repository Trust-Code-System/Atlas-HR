# Atlas HR Content Style Guide

## Voice

- Warm but expert. Like a senior HR friend explaining things over coffee.
- Confident, not hedgy. "You should" not "You might consider possibly."
- Plain language. No HR jargon without explaining it on first use.
- Specific. Real numbers, real examples, real company sizes.
- Inclusive. Globally aware. Do not assume the reader is in the US.

## Structure

- Open with a concrete scene or surprising fact, never "In today's world..."
- Use H2 for major sections (5-8 per article), H3 for subsections.
- Lead with the answer. Bury nothing.
- End with a "Key takeaways" bulleted summary.
- Use `<Callout>`, `<Steps>`, and `<Checklist>` liberally.

## Length

- Standard article: 1,500-2,200 words.
- Pillar article: 2,500-3,500 words.

## Examples that work

- "Sarah, an HR manager at a 200-person fintech in Berlin, faced this exact problem last year..."
- "Companies under 50 employees should aim for a time-to-hire under 21 days."
- "In Nigeria, statutory notice for permanent employees is one week per year of service, capped at one month."

## Examples that do not work

- "It is important to consider various factors..."
- "Different companies have different approaches..."
- "HR plays a crucial role in..."

## Disclaimers

- Always include `<Disclaimer />` at the end of articles touching termination, harassment, country-specific law, contracts, or pay equity.
- Articles about country-specific topics should cite the relevant statute by name if known, with a note that laws update.

## Tone for sensitive topics

- Terminations: humane, never callous, never euphemistic.
- Harassment: serious, victim-centered, procedurally clear.
- Mental health: empathetic, never minimizing.
